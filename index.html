The Coeur d'Alene Window Company

This employment is definitely the best testament to my current level of professionalism and maturity. There were a lot of factors as to why this happened, but the decision to earn my CDL prior and the sequence of events therein created the chance of experiencing this opportunity. And despite the fact that I am leaving this company, I will forever be grateful for everything.

I started July 9th, 2019. This was less than a month after I married my wonderful wife. The company CRST had sponsored my CDL education, but decided to fire me when I wouldn't answer the phone during my wedding. Considering how hectic their program was prior, I thought it wise to take the opportunity they gave me and find another company to work for. Sure enough, along came The Coeur d'Alene Window Company, and I am very grateful they gave me that opportunity.

Five and a half years allowed me several varieties of challenging experiences. I was also able to travel to several states and meet several interesting people. Every week it seemed there was something new I was learning or doing. I was also fortunate enough to train several new employees. This further helped me hone my craft, better understand how to troubleshoot problems, and find appropriate solutions that I could explain in a way others could understand.

Probably the number one lesson I learned with this company is the importance of communication. This particular issue created many interesting and challenging circumstances. I definitely had opportunities to practice troubleshooting issues, creating delivery game-plans, determining routing, analyzing costs, and other concerns that revolve around good common sense business practices. This, too, made me much more independent and proactive.

Here is a list of the type of tasks I was concerned with in this position:

  • Pre/Post-Trip Truck and Trailer
  • Communicate Issues Efficiently/Effectively
  • Check Accuracy of Load Paperwork
  • Check Load is Properly Secured
  • Route Myself Efficiently and Effectively
  • Find and Load Items
  • Various Yard Move Activities
  • Train New CDL Drivers
  • Accommodate Driving for Conditions
  • Chain Up When Necessary
  • Perform Intermittent Safety Checks
  • Maneuver Safely In and Out of Destinations
  • Unload Items With or Without Assistance
  • Accurately Document Delivery Information
  • Ensure Appropriate Remedy for Item Issues
  • Maintain Cleanliness of Truck and Trailer
  • Have defects fixed as occurs/approved

If someone were to look at the situation from the perspective of what CDL life is typically like, I think many fellow truck drivers would be envious. The pay was per hour, I would be able to get hotels and food on the company dime, my schedule would be typically consistent as Monday through Friday, and it had a decent balance of driving and physical labor. Leaving this company and this industry, though, came down to other factors.

When looking at truck driving, advancement leads to more road and less home. Truckers have to be where the loads are, and that can mean needing to be anywhere at any time. And once you're on the road, there's no end until the route or load is finished. Weather comes in, you fight through or hunker down. Truck breaks down, get have to get it fixed. You can't just decide to go home; it's an entire process and requires some type of sacrifice. Something big happens â like your wife giving birth â there is no âdrop-of-the-hatâ response. Doctor's appointments? Few and far between; if you get time. Meanwhile, declining health, terrible truck stop food, terrible sleep, and the only supplies you have are what you bring with you and what you have access to buy.

It was time. Time to find something different. Time to find something more progressive and challenging that could also afford me a much more healthy and fulfilling life. Thus, TripleTen and their Software Engineering program.

Retail work lost it's appeal, particularly since I was pushed into stocking shelves and had a part-time schedule. I wanted a more challenging environment with more potential for advancement. This lead me to McDonald's.

In comparison to Staples, McDonald's was much more fast-paced. There were plenty of tasks that I could focus on depending on what position I was for the day. That was the other aspect that was appealing: they moved me around. They weren't afraid to let me learn other positions. In fact, McDonald's was on track to have me open the store.

Although this experience ended in a short time-frame, it was the most intense and fulfilling work opportunity I had up to that time in my history. It was the first time I was able to see what it takes to actually run a business, and I still feel inspired to this day to start a restaurant once the finances allow it. It also triggered instant memorization and progressive inference, allowing me to act more and more quickly and accurately.

College was an interesting time for me. Although I was pushing for three Bachelors, I also allowed myself to be manipulated through peer pressure. It made life harder on myself, part of which included maintaining employment with companies. But even so, I still tried to push myself towards success. Staples was an excellent example of this.

This position was mainly cashiering. They would also have me help with stocking shelves and assisting customers on the floor. Sometimes I would help out the tech and copy departments depending. All in all, I would tend to float wherever I was needed if able. There was a time when I was considered for advancement; I would be the inventory manager. They had me take part in re-models of stores, have me practice with their software interfaces and processes, and all around get a taste of running the store.

Unfortunately, there were certain events that acted against that objective. These events made it where they wouldn't even have me as a cashier; I would just simply stock shelves. Now, there wasn't anything I did intentionally, but nevertheless the accumulation of events just made my future there ultimately dead. After I realized that, I searched for other work, which lead me to McDonald's.

I wanted to give recognition to my first employment. This was an entry level position as a âCourtesy Clerk,â which entailed bagging groceries, assisting customers, and gathering carts. It was a good start. At the time, I was dual-enrolling between high-school and college. This allowed me some extra cash and experience while hitting milestones like earning my driver's license.

I started the dual enrollment program in 2006 through North Idaho College (NIC). Other than the first semester, my classes were primary at the NIC campus. Originally, I was going to pursue Engineering through University of Idaho, which would have entailed transferring over the Moscow after earning my Associates. After evaluating costs, involving myself in stupidity as mentioned previously, and learning that Lewis-Clark State College (LCSC) was running their campus and programs right next to NIC, I decided to pursue Bachelors in Communications and Management.
